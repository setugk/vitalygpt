## Figma - Ways of working and Templates

Spotify organizes its UX design workflow by centralizing all design work for each product within a single Figma project owned by the product team, with individual feature pages serving as the single source of truth for design decisions across all designers and business units. Teams are structured around the products they build, and as designers refine their solutions, they move their work into centralized specification files to document decisions, eliminating confusion about which version is current. This organizational approach ensures there is only one authoritative place to reference design work, preventing designers from accessing outdated or incorrect information.

**Full post:**

Spotify Ways of Working (Figma Kit) (https://lnkd.in/ek9ZzZQg), an interesting case study on how Spotify organizes their design work — as long as designers from different business units and squads are designing for the same product, they are working together in the same space. Teams are organized by the products they build, projects are organized by product’s features and documentation (or specs) and are centralized inside a single project in each team. Each product team owns a single spec file, and pages within these files represent each feature. As a designer refines their solution, they eventually move their work into centralized specs to document their decisions — which is the source of truth for the entire organization. Ultimately, there is no “looking at the wrong thing”, because there is only one place to look. ## State of AI in 2025

AI products often fail because designers envision systems that can't be reliably built while data scientists create solutions searching for problems, resulting in a significant product-market-fit gap. Current AI limitations include 85-90% accuracy at best, hallucinations, context forgetfulness, and heavy reliance on high-quality data, meaning AI works best for first drafts and augmenting human decision-making rather than autonomous operation. Successful AI integration focuses on practical, grounded applications using AI as an invisible UI component (through controls and panels rather than flashy text boxes) where humans remain in the loop to review and confirm outputs. As synthetic training data becomes prevalent and AI becomes more integrated into everyday products, the key to viable AI products lies in solving real problems with appropriate human oversight rather than pursuing impressive but impractical capabilities.

## AI Chat UX

Vitaly Friedman outlines essential design patterns for AI chat interfaces, emphasizing that transparency about AI identity is crucial for building user trust—disguising AI as human erodes confidence. Key recommendations include offering human support options, using distinct avatars for AI and humans, collapsing older conversations, allowing users to adjust reasoning detail levels, and selecting appropriate interface layouts (full-page for complex tasks, side panels for co-creation, widgets for quick tasks). The article highlights that people naturally interact differently with AI than humans—using shorter messages, avoiding politeness, and being more direct—so honest disclosure of AI interactions actually improves communication and strengthens customer relationships.

**Full post:**

🧔🏽‍♂️ Design Patterns For AI Chat Interfaces. With practical guidelines on how to designing more useful, and less annoying AI chat ↓ 🚫 Nothing erodes trust more than disguised AI.
🤔 Often users dismiss AI chats almost instinctively. ✅ Users expect an option to “speak to human”.
✅ Be transparent about who users speak to.
✅ Wait for users to end a chat on their terms. ✅ Use separate avatars for AI bot and humans.
✅ Context changes over time: collapse older chats.
✅ Support pinning chats + highlight useful bits.
✅ Let users adjust granularity of reasoning traces.
✅ Allow users to restore iterations of canvases. ✅ Allow users to collapse chat without ending it.
✅ For long, complex tasks → full page screen.
✅ For multi-tasking, co-creation → side panel.
✅ For short, momentary tasks → chat widget.
✅ On mobile, full page AI chat works best. As many product teams race to not fall behind AI features in their products, we see AI chat interfaces becoming almost second nature every time AI initiative is launched. However, people have difficulty articulating intent in a chat, and good old UI controls — buttons, presets, radio buttons could help there. Nothing erodes trust more than an AI that desperately pretend to be a human. We might not be able to distinguish AI-generated content from human-crafted content, but human conversations differ significantly from AI chats — and there people spot the difference almost immediately: – People talk in quick bursts of text → AI is verbose (by default).
– People can respond with 1–2 words → AI respond with sentences.
– People never receive unfinished text → AI “streams” output live.
– Messages can arrive unprompted → AI responds to prompts.
– People have strong opinions → AI is apologetic, overcorrects itself Knowing that AI is on the other side isn’t really a problem — it’s what is required to build trust. And when people realize they talk to a chatbot, they are more direct, use “keyword” language, and avoid politeness markers. But when service does provide access to humans, it shows that company cares about customers. Human answer might not be accurate or swift, but it builds a much stronger relationship with the brand, especially when things go unexpected ways. 💎 Useful resources: Visa Design System: Chat UI Patterns

## Figma file organization

This article presents a systematic approach to organizing 1,250+ design screens in Figma by dividing products into files based on navigation sections, with each section containing pages organized by user flows. The methodology emphasizes ordering screens left-to-right for progression and top-to-bottom for variations, while using color-coded file covers (blue for work-in-progress, green for validated versions, and grey for archived files) to communicate status. Rather than designing flows intuitively in isolation, this approach comprehensively explores both happy and unhappy user paths within a unified structure, reducing redundant work while maintaining organization at both the design system and user flow levels.

## Figma cleanliness

This guide provides best practices for organizing and maintaining clean Figma design files. Key recommendations include using sections to separate content, implementing hierarchical naming conventions with slashes, creating dedicated pages for components and iterations, and replacing groups with frames for better structure. The post also emphasizes version history tracking, creating file thumbnails, and includes links to additional resources on Figma organization and component library management.

🧹 How To Clean Up A Messy Figma File. How to keep your Figma designs clean — with clever ideas for neat setup and better maintenance (check contributors and guides below ↓) ✅ Use sections (Shift+S) to separate happy path from edge cases.
✅ Use ”/” in your naming conventions, e.g. Button / Primary.
✅ Use blank pages ”*****” or “______” or ”------” to create chapters.
✅ Bulk name layers by pressing ⌘+R/Ctrl+R with all of them selected.
✅ Use Ctrl/⌘ + R for naming every time you create a new layer, frame etc. ✅ Whenever you repeat yourself a few times, create a style for it.
✅ Use sections: they don’t show in presentation mode; only frames do.
✅ Create a thumbnail for each of your files to make them easier to find.
✅ Use “Save to version history” (⌘+⌥+S / Ctrl+Alt+S) to track changes.
🚫 Stop using groups: only use frames for resizing, content overflow etc. 🖼️ Cover page: key information and the table of contents of the file.

## Practical guide to designing icons

This practical guide to icon design provides essential guidelines for creating consistent icons in design systems and UIs, including using an 8-pixel grid, maintaining uniform sizes, aligning objects to the pixel grid, and choosing between filled or stroked styles based on use case. Key principles for visual balance emphasize trusting your eye over mathematics, using the blur test for evaluation, and understanding that squares visually outweigh circles and triangles, which should be drawn larger or wider for balance. The resource compiles curated learning materials organized into three categories: foundational icon design courses and templates, practical guides for designing well-balanced icons and understanding optical effects, and advanced topics on scaling icon systems in design environments like Spotify.

🪴 A Practical Guide To Designing Icons (+ Figma templates). Guidelines and kits for icons in design systems and UIs ↓ ✅ Use the 8-pixel grid to build icons at 16, 24 or 32px.
✅ All icons should be the same size when you build them.
✅ Start adding sizes when you need more detail/complexity.
✅ Start with the largest size and then go down from there.
✅ Use 1 color for UI icons (black), 2 colors for brand. ✅ Always align objects to the pixel grid to stay consistent.
✅ For optical grid, add padding at the edge = stroke weight.
✅ Use filled icons for easier recognition, stroked for tiny details. ✅ For border, use sharp corner, 0.5px or 2px corner radius.

✅ The easiest test for visual balance is by blurring the items.
✅ In same size, squares always outweigh circles and triangles.

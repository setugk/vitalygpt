## Getting work recognized

This article advocates for maintaining a "brag document" to record accomplishments, wins, and impact in your design career, as good work often gets overlooked due to organizational changes, management transitions, and competing priorities. The document should be updated regularly (weekly or bi-weekly) and include concrete evidence such as new skills, projects completed, team initiatives, mentoring work, and lessons learned. Key tips include avoiding bulk updates, linking accomplishments to the next career level to strengthen promotion cases, and accumulating measurable outcomes. The article provides several free Notion templates and resources to help professionals track and document their professional achievements.

**Full post:**

How to record your accomplishments, small and big wins and the impact you’ve made with your design — to build confidence and better progress in your career ↓ We often assume that good work speaks for itself. If we just work hard enough, our work will get noticed and we will be elevated across our career ladder. Yet more often than not, your achievements will get lost somewhere between reorg efforts, new priorities, abandoned initiatives and urgent deadlines. Managers change all the time. You might have a strong relationship with your manager already, but never get a chance to move up the ladder because they have already moved to another team. A new manager, despite all your efforts, often won’t be able to promote you as an internal policy might block any new promotions in their first 6 or 12 months. So you’ll have to start over again. A good way to push back is to have a “brag document” (https://lnkd.in/ekntJPYU) — a running document that lists your small and big achievements, feedback from your managers and colleagues, screenshots of your appraisals and recommendations, along with lessons you’ve learned. It also builds confidence in your abilities and helps you better see your career trajectory. Useful things to include: – New skills you’ve acquired
– New certificates from UX training
– Projects that saved teams time
– Workshops you’ve organized
– Onboarding sessions you helped with
– Team-wide changes you’ve initiated
– Endorsements you’ve received
– Mentoring sessions you’ve coached
– How you successfully changed culture
– How you’ve dealt with design debt
– Proposing, getting buy-in, delivering
– Coordinating complex comms
– Lessons you’ve learned
– Conflicts you’ve resolved Useful pointers to keep in mind: 🚫 Don’t update in huge and bulky batches.
✅ Update it regularly, weekly or bi-weekly.
✅ Set a recurring event on your calendar.
✅ Accumulate concrete evidence and results.
✅ Work also happens outside of actual design work.

If you can prove that you’ve been performing at the next level for past 3-6 months, you will make the case for your promotion strong and more obvious. ## Guide to UX Job Interviews

UX job interviews typically consist of 4-7 sessions where companies assess candidates across six key areas: product thinking, interaction design, visual design, intentionality, enthusiasm, and self-awareness. Success requires thorough preparation—researching the company, demonstrating a V-shaped skill set (deep expertise in one area with broad knowledge of related fields), and showcasing business acumen, accessibility understanding, and technical proficiency. Candidates should be honest about their goals and strengths while structuring case studies using the STAR method to demonstrate preparedness and serious interest in the opportunity. Most importantly, interviews seek potential, genuine interest, and a willingness to learn and deliver impactful work.

**Full post:**

🧘🏽‍♀️ A Complete Guide To UX Job Interviews (https://lnkd.in/eUZp5FBa), a very (!) thorough overview of the design interview process — from silent interviews and screener interviews to practicing, interview loop and product critique. Neatly put together by Aaron James. The interview process typically includes 4–7 interview sessions. In these interviews, company wants to get to know you better. They will explore if you’re planning on changing jobs soon, if you are ready for relocation, and what your expectations are. Usually they will ask about your strengths and weaknesses. The best thing you can do is to be honest. Do your homework. Study the company, the team and what they do before you even have the first call, and have an answer to the question where you think you could contribute best. Usually they aren’t looking for a T-shaped employee who specializes in one topic, but has little knowledge about everything else. Really they are looking for a V-shaped employee — one who has a deep knowledge of a subject matter, but also a broad understanding of neighbouring areas. But most importantly, they are looking for potential, excitement, genuine interest and willingness to learn and do your best work. In general, you need to demonstrate strengths in 6 areas: product thinking, interaction design, visual design, intentionality, enthusiasm and self-awareness. Bonus points if you can show that you understand the business side of things as well (from cutting costs to legacy pains to design KPIs to risk management), and that you need to make sure that your design decisions have a visible impact on business metrics. If you can also show that you are well-versed in accessibility and technical aspects, that’s a bingo right there. Finally, some managers are almost obsessed with the STAR method for presenting a case study. You don’t need to mention it, but you need to structure it in that way, as they see it as a sign of how seriously you take the opportunity and how well you are prepared. It can be nerve-wrecking, but it’s really a matter of practice and preparation. ## First 90 days

The collection includes checklists, Notion templates, and practical advice from industry experts on structuring the first 90 days. Key recommendations emphasize identifying bottlenecks that slow down teams, asking questions, conducting effective one-on-ones, and defining success metrics at 30, 60, and 90-day intervals. The primary goal is to help new designers find their fit within their teams and discover opportunities to add real value while building meaningful professional relationships.

## Presentation design

Vitaly Friedman shares key principles for creating impactful presentations, emphasizing treating slides as a support to your spoken words rather than a replacement, showing personality, and maintaining audience engagement through pacing and eye contact. He highlights actionable takeaways from "The Art Of Deck Making" workshop, including skipping obvious content, using silence effectively, and remembering that audiences want speakers to succeed. Friedman reflects on his own journey from giving poor presentations to developing a systematic approach to deck design through typography, storytelling, and removing unnecessary elements. He encourages aspiring presenters that with practice, patience, and authenticity, they can create compelling presentations that resonate with audiences.

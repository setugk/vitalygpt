## 🥊 Top vs. Side Navigation In Complex Products (https://lnkd.in/e54HWryC), with interesting thoughts on which 3-level menu navigation structure works better for a complex product, and how to decide…

This article examines the trade-offs between top and side navigation for complex products. Top navigation maximizes vertical space on wide desktop displays but can hide options behind menus, while side navigation allows users to see more options at once and enables faster vertical scanning, though it consumes horizontal space. The author recommends deciding between the two by asking three key questions: how much space is needed for content, how many top-level navigation items exist, and what tasks users perform most frequently. Ultimately, both approaches can work effectively when backed by strong information architecture that prioritizes accessibility of frequently-used features and avoids hindering task completion.

## Guide to tree testing

Tree testing is a reliable UX method for validating navigation early in the design process, before creating any visual designs. The process involves conducting card sorting with 15-30 users to understand their mental models, then setting up 10-12 representative tasks in tools like Treejack or UserZoom and testing with 25-50 participants across 3-4 rounds to achieve an 80% success rate. Key metrics to track include directness, time spent, first click, and destination, while stakeholder sign-offs throughout the process ensure alignment and build confidence. Once high success rates are achieved, the navigation structure can be expanded to include secondary levels, with the understanding that users ultimately care more about reaching the right destination page than the exact navigation labels.

One detail that often gets overlooked is to regularly run check-ins and get sign-offs from stakeholders — throughout the entire process. There must be an agreement that selected tasks indeed accurately represent the product to avoid turnarounds down the line. Plus, by getting green light, you also build some confidence and trust early on. Trust me: you will need it.

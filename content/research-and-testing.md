## UX Brainstorming

Booking.com shares a structured approach to UX ideation that replaces assumption-based brainstorming with research-driven insights. The method involves clustering data points into user needs, business goals, and competitive insights, then reframing themes as "How Might We" statements to focus teams on specific problems and desired outcomes. By combining these three pillars and prioritizing ideas through visual organization and storytelling, teams can generate realistic, viable ideas with intentional structure while maintaining creative flexibility. A Miro template is provided to facilitate rapid ideation sessions that benefit from cross-functional participation.

## Journey maps vs Service Blueprints

User journey maps and service blueprints are complementary UX tools that visualize different aspects of customer experiences. User journey maps document the customer's front-stage experience through their interactions and emotional responses, while service blueprints expose the back-stage internal operations, technology, and processes that enable those experiences. Together, these mapping methods help organizations identify and fix broken parts of both the user experience and internal operations. The article provides free Figma templates and practical guides for implementing both approaches in human-centered design.

**Full post:**

🗺️ User Journey Maps vs. Service Blueprints (+ Figma Templates) (https://lnkd.in/d8tNmKe2), a fantastic article explaining differences between the two, when to use each, along with a free practical guide to get started. Kindly put together by Morgan Miller and Erika Flowers. As Morgan and Erika write, mapping experiences is a key part of a human-centered business. We need to look at both perspectives — what the person experiences (UX, front stage), and what went on outside of their view to make it happen (Service Design, backstage). With user journey maps, we visualize and document user’s experience. We interview customers to capture their insights, then map patterns. We list steps and actions they go through to meet their goals — sometimes with storyboards, or Jobs-to-Be-Done, or emotional responses. The outcome is an aggregate, real-world experience (front stage) — framed as a narrative. Those user journeys often start way before users start interacting with your product — so we need to include non-digital touch points as well. Customer journey maps are just like user journey maps, just for a different persona: e.g. in B2B, customers might not be end users. Service blueprints are not about documenting the user experience. They apply user experience as starting point, and unpack it to expose how it is *internally* created — with technology, people, operations, processes involved (backstage). Journey maps and service blueprints highlight different sides of the experience story. But they have one thing in common: they help us understand the broken parts and fix them. The outcome, then, is a great UX and great internal processes that shape and enable it. Useful resources: Guide to Journey Maps + Templates, by Stéphanie Walter

## Writing better UX research reports

A practical guide to writing effective UX research reports emphasizes structuring findings to be persuasive and engaging through a story arc that includes background and goals, methods, insights, and actionable recommendations. Rather than dense text, reports should showcase video clips, verbatim quotes, and direct observations that help stakeholders understand not just what users said, but why they said it and what implications it has for the product and business. Key sections include documenting research methods to build stakeholder trust, focusing on 3-5 striking and memorable insights, and concluding with actionable next steps that connect short-term objectives to long-term strategic goals. This comprehensive approach helps UX researchers explain user behavior and persuade teams to act on findings.

**Full post:**

🗃️ How To Write Better UX Research Reports (https://lnkd.in/eZ2aGwkU), a practical guide on how to structure findings from a UX research study more effectively, to be persuasive, engaging and use a story arc — from background and goals, to methods, activities, insights, findings and actionable recommendations. Neatly put together by Allison Grayce Marshall. I absolutely love Allison’s point that good UX research presents findings and insights, but doesn’t stop there. It helps teams understand what people said, why they said it and what implications those findings have on the product and the business. It explains behavior and persuades stakeholders. 🎯 Background and Goals
First, we reflect the current state of things back to stakeholders. We explain who conducted research, the goals and scope of the project — both for current research activities and any research that will be conducted in the future. 🔬 Methods and Activities
Next, we document how we uncovered insights, and why we chose specific methods and activities to do so. We showcase why insights are statistically significant, and whenever possible, highlight sample size needed for reliable surveys or interviews, confidence level and margin for error. We need it to build trust and confidence with stakeholders. 📣 Video Snippets and Verbatim Quotes
Instead of using dense text paragraphs, we show video and audio clips, interview snippets and photos taken — not interpretations, but verbatim quotes, and direct observations. We tell the story that covers the journey a user went through, where they struggled and succeeded. In fact, we can use existing artefacts such as journey maps to visualize what exactly a user is going through, and what we’ve learned by observing these journeys. 🔮 Findings and Insights
Next, we focus on key insights — ways of thinking, wrong assumptions, mismatch of expectations. We want to focus on 3–5 key findings that are striking, easy to remember and act upon. Bonus points if we can also connect them with risks, costs and missed opportunities. 🚀 Actionable Next Steps
